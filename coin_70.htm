Features:

Reportedly from the Gussage All Saints hoard, as indicated by an older ticket describing the coin, though not traced to any of the known coins from the hoard

The Gussage All Saints hoard of 662 late Roman silver coins was found on 21 March 2010 by Mr I. West using a metal detector on ploughed land in the parish of Gussage All Saints, Dorset, in southwest England. The hoard was contained in a small earthenware flagon, which was lifted and then taken to the British Museum in London where the coins were then removed, conserved and catalogued, following the application of the 1996 Treasure Act as Treasure case No. 2010T177. The find was recorded in the Portable Antiquities Scheme (PAS; see https://finds.org.uk) with record IDs DOR-A1CCB1 and IARCH-8D9AB1. It was also included in the list of coin hoards published in 2011 in The Numismatic Chronicle, volume 171, pp. 407–24, as item No. 54. The hoard comprised 653 siliquae and 9 miliarenses. Curiously the miliarenses were found at the top, in the neck of the flagon, thereby suggesting careful packing of the coins into the pot at the time of concealment. The earliest coins were issues of Constantius II (r. 337-361), struck in the period 355-361, and the latest coins were issues of Arcadius (r. 383-408) and Honorius (r. 393-423) from the Milan mint struck in the period 395-402. In addition to coins of these Roman emperors, coins of all the Roman emperors in the period 355-402, Julian (as Caesar from 355 to 360 and Augustus from 361 to 363), Jovian (r. 363-364), Valentinian I (r. 364-375), Valens (r. 364-378), Gratian (r. 367-383), Valentinian II (r. 375-392), Theodosius I (r. 379-395), Magnus Maximus (usurper; r. 383-388), Flavius Victor (usurper; r. 383-388), and Flavius Eugenius (usurper; r. 392-394) were all represented. A small number of coins could not be attributed with certainty to a specific emperor and a few others were unidentifiable issues.

The composition of the hoard by emperor and denomination was: Constantius II (22 siliquae), Julian (52 siliquae and 1 miliarense), Jovian (5 siliquae), Valentinian I (10 siliquae and 1 miliarense), Valens (76 siliquae and 3 miliarenses), Gratian (68 siliquae and 2 miliarenses), Valentinian II (38 siliquae), Theodosius I (70 siliquae), Magnus Maximus (72 siliquae and 1 miliarense), Flavius Victor (9 siliquae), Flavius Eugenius (33 siliquae and 1 miliarense), Arcadius (102 siliquae), Honorius (65 siliquae), and unidentified (31 siliquae, of which 1 from the House of Constantine, 13 from the House of Theodosius and 1 from the House of Valentinian).

The majority of the coins were from the Augusta Treverorum (now Trier; 351 coins) and Mediolanum (now Milan; 136 coins) mints. Other western Roman Empire mints represented were Lugdunum (now Lyon; 45 coins), Arelate (now Arles; 44 coins), Roma (now Rome; 27 coins), and Aquileia (17 coins). In addition there were a small number of eastern Roman Empire mints with Siscia (3 coins), Sirmium (3 coins), Thessalonica (1 coin), Constantinople (4 coins), Nicomedia (2 coins) and Antioch (6 coins).

The composition of the Gussage All Saints hoard is comparable to the much larger and more famous Hoxne hoard, found on 16 November 1992 in Hoxne, Suffolk, in east England. In comparison, the Gussage All Saints hoard has a higher percentage of coins of Trier as opposed to Milan and a higher proportion dating from 378-395, perhaps reflecting its slightly earlier deposition than the Hoxne hoard. Many of the coins have been subject to a low level of clipping, a practice common to British found Siliquae of this period; however the extent of clipping is far less severe than that noted for the Hoxne hoard. This, along with the content of the hoard, suggests a date of deposition of circa 410. The hoard therefore dates from the period when the Roman Empire in the West, beset with the collapse of the Rhine frontier and invasions in Gaul and Italy, relinquished its authority over the British province which was left to its own devices and increasingly vulnerable to Germanic and Irish raiding.

On completion of the Treasure process, one coin (siliqua of Valentinian II from the Siscia mint) was acquired by the British Museum, and the rest of the coins along with the earthenware flagon in which the hoard was found were returned to the finder, who brought them to Spink in 2012. Spink offered each variety of the miliarenses, a selection of the best of the siliquae, a few "large lots" and the flagon at auction on 26 September 2012.

The composition of the hoard revealed 47 siliquae of Theodosius I from the Trier mint struck in the period 388-392/395. These coins include all known varieties (RIC9#94B, RIC9#95B and RIC9#106A). The Spink auction offered some of these coins: 1 as individual lot (Lot 425) and a maximum of 7 as part of a "large lot" (Lot 457). Further coins were acquired by coin dealers, including Antiqua, Ltd., who put some of them for sale in their fixed price Catalogue XVII in 2013, and Roma Numismatics Ltd. However, I have not found any trace of the present coin in auction records that report the Gussage All Saints hoard as provenance.

Note:

Constantine I (the Great; r. 307–337) made further changes to the monetary system that was reformed by Diocletian (r. 284-305) circa 294. In place of the gold aureus, which was currently being struck 60 to the pound, he introduced into the western provinces a new and lighter coin called the solidus, which was struck 72 to the pound and hence was weighing, in Greco-Roman units, 24 siliquae or carats. The weight of the Roman pound is normally reckoned at 327.45 g, giving the solidus a theoretical weight in modern terms of 4.55 g. With the defeat of Constantine I’s eastern rival Licinius (r. 308–324) production of the solidus became universal throughout the Roman Empire. The aureus was still occasionally struck thereafter, but its issue was generally confined to the celebration of special occasions. Two gold fractional denominations accompanied the solidus, though they were never produced in the same quantities as the larger piece. The semissis was the equivalent of a half solidus (12 siliquae), while the 9-siliqua piece (also called the one and a half scripulum) was the equivalent of three-eighths of a solidus. In 383 Theodosius I introduced the gold tremissis, or one-third solidus (8 siliquae), which quickly replaced the curious and seemingly inconvenient 9-siliqua piece. The tremissis became one of the commonest denominations in the 5th century and beyond.

In 325 Constantine I resumed production of the siliqua (or argenteus), which had been introduced by Diocletian and had lapsed after about 310. At the same time he introduced the larger silver miliarense, which was one-third heavier than the siliqua (four scrupula instead of three) and the same weight as the gold solidus (4.55 g). A few years later, after the death of Constantine I, a heavier version of the miliarense was introduced. This "heavy miliarenses" was struck 60 to the pound, the same weight as the old gold aureus (5.46 g). Both versions of the miliarense remained in issue over a considerable period of time, extending even into the period of the Byzantine Empire. The siliqua, however, soon underwent some fundamental changes. About 357 Constantius II (r. 337-361), the last surviving son of Constantine I, reduced the weight of the siliqua from 96 to the pound (about 3.41 g or three scrupula) to 144 to the pound (about 2.27 grams or two scrupula). In consequence, the lighter version of the miliarense now became a double siliqua.
Historical notes:

(Flavius) Theodosius I (born on 11 January 347 at Cauca in Gallaecia in the north-west of Spain; proclaimed emperor on 19 January 379; died on 17 January 395 at Mediolanum)

(To the right) St. Ambrose barring Theodosius from Milan Cathedral, painted by Sir Anthony van Dyck (1599-1641) circa 1619-1620. This painting depicts St. Ambrose (circa 339-397), bishop of Milan, stopping the emperor Theodosius I (circa 346–395) and his retinue from entering Milan Cathedral, as punishment for his massacre of the people of Thessalonica, who had murdered the Gothic "magister militum" Butheric (?-390) in 390.

Flavius Theodosius was the namesake son of the celebrated principal general, appointed "magister equitum" by Valentinian I (364-375) from 369 until his execution at Carthage circa 375. Theodosius thus had little reason to love the House of Valentinian and this may well have influenced his future behaviour. On his father's downfall, the future emperor retired to his Spanish estates and settled down to private life. He married Aella Flaccilla (circa 355-386), a fellow Spaniard, about 376. The following year a son, Arcadius (circa 377-408), was born to the couple, and a second son, Honorius (384-423), was added eight years later.

With the death of Valens (364-378), the Roman emperor in the East, in the catastrophic defeat by the Goths of the eastern Roman army at Hadrianopolis (on 9 August 378), Gratian (367-383), the Roman Emperor in the West, was faced with a dilemma. Clearly the throne of Constantinople needed to be filled promptly by a competent replacement. Gratian's younger brother and co-emperor Valentinian II (375-392) was still only eight-year old and obviously unequal to the daunting task. For the time being, Gratian decided to recall Theodosius from his provincial retirement and place him in command of the seriously threatened Danube frontier, with the rank of "magister equitum", which his father had borne under the rule of Valentinian I. He had already gained considerable military experience on his father's staff during the latter's brilliant campaigns against the Picts and the Scots in Britain in 368. Having achieved a measure of success over the invaders, Theodosius was summoned to Sirmium where he was invested by Gratian with the imperial purple and appointed ruler of the Roman emperor in the East in 379. The dynasty which he founded was to rule the eastern Roman Empire for the following seven decades, though unfortunately it did not produce any other rulers of note throughout this period.

The sixteen-year reign of Theodosius I was much troubled by western usurpations, from Magnus Maximus (383-388) and his son (Flavius) Victor (383-388) and Flavius Eugenius (392-394), the quelling of both of them required the emperor's personal intervention. Gratian had been overthrown by Magnus Maximus in the summer of 383 and the young Valentinian II was hardly in a position to avenge his brother. Theodosius, whose relations with Gratian had been steadily deteriorating, may not have been sorry to witness his removal from the political scene at the hands of a third party. Initially he tolerated the rule of the usurper in most of the western provinces and even struck some very rare coin issues in his name in the East. The authority of Valentinian II, the legitimate Roman Emperor in the West, was mostly confined to Italy, a state of affairs which prevailed until 387. At this point Magnus Maximus, clearly wishing to make himself sole Roman Emperor in the West and an equal partner with Theodosius I, invaded the peninsula. Valentinian II and his family were obliged to flee to Thessalonica and place themselves under the protection of Theodosius I. During this time Theodosius I met and fell in love with Valentinian II's sister Galla (circa 365-394) and the imperial nuptials were celebrated in 388 in return for a promise by Theodosius I to invade Italy and remove Magnus Maximus from power. Good to his word, Theodosius I defeated his adversary at Aquileia and executed him shortly thereafter. He may have been reluctant to do this as Magnus Maximus was a kinsman and would have been a far more competent western colleague than Valentinian II, but he made the sacrifice for the sake of his new wife. Valentinian II now returned to the West, but instead of becoming his brother-in-law's full colleague in the government of the Roman Empire, his authority was now restricted to Gaul. Theodosius I considered him too inexperienced to take on this responsibility and placed him under the guardianship of the Frankish "magister militum" Arbogastes (circa 375-394). Theodosius I remained in Italy for some three years while he endeavoured to settle affairs in the western Roman Empire, long subject to the rule of an usurper, but in 391 he returned to Constantinople. Arbogastes saw this as an opportunity to assert his own claims to power and his arrogant behaviour towards the now 21-year-old Valentinian II led the young man to despair of the hopelessness of his situation and to take his own life in 392. After an "interregnum" of over three months Arbogastes now nominated Flavius Eugenius, a former teacher of Latin grammar and rhetoric, to be his puppet-emperor. The empress Galla, obviously much distressed at news of her brother's death, urged her husband to take prompt action against the rebel regime. Perhaps sensing that his health was beginning to fall, Theodosius I raised his ten-year-old younger son Honorius to the rank of Augustus before organising another invasion of the West. On 6 September 394 he defeated the combined forces of the usurpers, probably at the battle of the Frigidus in Pannonia; Flavius Eugenius was executed and Arbogastes took his own life. Little more than four months later, Theodosius I himself died of dropsy (oedema) in his fiftieth year, leaving his inexperienced and incompetent sons Arcadius and Honorius to divide the Empire between them.

An accurate assessment of the abilities and achievements of Theodosius I is rendered difficult by the complexity of his character and the many seeming inconsistencies in his actions. A deeply devout Christian, he championed the cause of orthodoxy and was very intolerant towards "heretical" beliefs. It seems that the title "the Great" was due to his devout Christianity rather than having a secular basis. His religious views were greatly influenced by the powerful bishop Ambrose of Milan (circa 339-397), a patron saint of Milan and of beekeepers, and pagans and paganism received particularly severe treatment. He refused to allow the restoration of the Altar of Victory, which had been removed from the Roman Curia (Senate House) by Gratian in 382. Great controversy surrounded this issue and the altar's removal from its traditional home (since the time of Augustus) became symbolic of the final demise of paganism. It was briefly restored to its place under the usurper Flavius Eugenius but after that there is no further record of it in history. Theodosius I was the last ruler to exercise effective control over the entire Empire and the era of decline was ushered in with the succession in 395 of his two sons, Arcadius in the East and Honorius in the West, neither of whom had any aptitude for leadership.
