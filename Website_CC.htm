By the middle of Henry III's reign the "Short Cross" coinage in circulation was in a poor state and, in 1247, a new "Long Cross" coinage was ordered with the cross on the reverse being extended to the edge of the coin in an attempt to prevent clipping. The earliest coins (Class 1a) showed the name of neither the mint nor the moneyer. Class 1b includes the name of the mint. From Class 2 onwards all coins show the name of both the mint and the moneyer.

Following publication of the 1908 Brussels Hoard by Churchill and Thomas (2012) the classification of the "Long Cross" coinage was revised. This divided the "Long Cross" coinage into four phases: Phase I (1247, pre-provincial), Phase II (1248-1250, provincial, when sixteen provincial mints, in addition to the main mints of London and Canterbury plus Bury St Edmunds were opened to facilitate the production and distribution of the new coins), Phase III (1250-1272, post-provincial, in which the mint of Durham re-opened, operating intermittently), and Phase IV (1272-1280, posthumous, when the "Long Cross" coinage was continued in the name of Henry III through the first seven years of the reign of Edward I).

In Phases I and II the obverse features the Henry III's head without a sceptre (Classes 1-3) but during Phase III the sceptre was re-introduced (Class 4 onwards). No round hald-pennies ir farthings were struck during the "Long Cross" coinage but cut halves and quarters were used in considerable numbers.

Mint marks features in all coins from Class 1 to 4 and are an important aid to both chronology and identification. Five main varieties of mint mark exist, all in the shape of a star.

In 215 Caracalla reformed the monetary system to finance ever increasing imperial expenditures by introducing the antoninianus, named after Caracalla's official name "Antoninus". The newly introduced antoninianus had the same silver purity as the denarius (51.5%) but weighed 5.1 g, compared to 3.23 g for the denarius. Although its silver content was only equivalent to about one and a half denarii, the antoninianus was apparently officially tariffed as the equivalent of two. For this reason, it is sometimes referred to as "double-denarius". This new coin always shows the emperor wearing the radiate crown instead of the laurel wreath of the denarius. In the case of empresses, the coin is distinguished by the addition of a crescent placed beneath the bust. It was initially mostly silver, but was slowly debased, following the trend of the debasement of silver during the Severan dynasty (193-235), to bronze with a minimal silver content (see Pense, 1992). Elagabalus demonetised the antoninianus in 219 in favour of the denarius. The antoninianus was re-introduced in 238 by Gordian III who, in turn, saw to it intentionally or otherwise that the denarius would be phased out for good.

During the years 247 and 248 the "Saeculares" coinage struck to commemorate the 1000th anniversary of the founding of Rome included the following reverse inscriptions: "SAECLARES AVGG" (the age of the Two Emperors), "SAECVLVM NOVVM" (the new age) and "MILIARIVM SAECVLVM" (the millenia). The "Saeculares" coinage is divided into two reverse series: the reverse animal series showing wild animals (with the inscription "SAECLARES AVGG") and the reverse architectural series illustrating various temples (with the inscription "SAECVLVM NOVVM") and cippus (columns) (with the inscription "MILIARIVM SAECVLVM"). The reverse animal series shows at least eight different wild animals: a lion, a she-wolf (suckling the twins Romulus and Remus), a gazelle, a European elk (moose), a hippopotamus, a stag, a goat (or hind), and an antelope. Struck in a wide range of denominations the series included the gold aureus, the silver antoninianius and quinarius, and the bronze sestertius, dupondius and as. Included also are issues of Otacilia Severa, wife of Philip I the Arab, and Philip (Il) the Younger, their son who had been raised to the rank of Augustus in 247.

The "Ludi Saeculares" (Secular Games) organised for the 1000th anniversary of the founding of Rome was highlighted by a splendid array of wild animals. These wild animals had originally been collected for the intended triumph of Gordian III in the Persian war, which saw his death and the associated proclamation of Philip (I) the Arab as emperor in 244. The animals were used by Philip (I) the Arab in magnificent displays and (slaughter) games put on in the key venues, like the Circus Maximus and the Colosseum. This large collection of exotic wild beasts included several specimens of antelope, elephant, European elk (moose), gazelle, giraffe, goat, hippopotamus, wild horse, hyena, leopard, lion, rhinoceros, stag, tiger and wild ass. It was these animals, collected for Gordian III, which, no doubt, inspired the many animal reverse types found on the "Saeculares" coinage struck to commemorate the 1000th anniversary of the founding of Rome. Besides, the she-wolf (suckling the twins Romulus and Remus) would have been recognised as the symbol of Rome rather than as a reminder of the games' arena.

Struck in a wide range of denominations the series included the gold aureus, the silver antoninianius and quinarius, and the bronze sestertius, dupondius and as. Included also are issues of Otacilia Severa, wife of Philip I the Arab, and Philip Il, their eleven year old son who had been raised to the rank of Augustus. The mint at Rome striking the "Saeculares" coinage was apparently divided into six officinae (workshops), which are of much interest in that the eight animals illustrated were divided among them with Officina I striking only the lion, II the lion and the she-wolf, III the gazelle and the European elk (moose), IV the hippopotamus, V the stag, and VI the goat (or hind) and the antelope.
