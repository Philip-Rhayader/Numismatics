Note:

For several centuries before the rise of Rome there had been a tradition of featuring animals, birds, fish and insects (as well as various mythological beasts) on the coinages of many of the Greek city states. Rome inherited this tradition, and although the representation of fauna is less frequent and varied than on coins of the Greek series, they nevertheless form a most appealing group within the Roman coinage.

Crocodile, heifer, bull, wild boar, lion attacking stag, eagle, crab and butterfly, capricorn, Pegasus and Sphinx all appear on the coinage of Augustus, who was the inheritor of the late Republican tradition of great diversity in the selection of coin types. However, during the course of his long reign that tradition was gradually superseded by a more conservative approach to the type content of the new Imperial coinage. Accordingly, the coinages of the later Julio-Claudian emperors feature virtually no representations of animals, other than the elephants drawing the car of Divus Augustus on a sestertius issued by Tiberius, and the eagle appearing on the reverse of a Divus Augustus as.

The Flavian revival of earlier coin types led to a reintroduction of the tradition of animal depiction on the Imperial coinage. Several types issued in 78 draw from country life. A reverse shows a goat being milked by a goat-herd and another has a sow, with three of her youngs (piglets). These agricultural scenes may be seen as the outline of a programme for the restauration of agricultural prosperity in Italy, of which no detailed record is known. It is worth nothing that little is known about the morphology of domestic pigs and their forms of keeping in ancient Graeco-Roman times from written sources. The depictions of domestic pigs on ancient artefacts such as coins provide important sources of information (see Meyer et al., 2004).

In the 2nd century the Pegasus and the griffin appear on several aes denominations of Hadrian whilst his successor Antoninus Pius struck asses showing an elephant and a sow suckling its young beneath an oak tree, both types probably having reference to the celebrations connected with the 900th anniversary of the foundation of Rome. An attractive representation of a dove appears on an aureus of Antoninus Pius' daughter, the younger Faustina, and elephants occur on an as of Commodus and a denarius of Septimius Severus.

The "king of beasts" is depicted on the coinage of Caracalla, wearing a radiate crown and holding a thunderbolt in its jaws, and several decades later the lion reappears as part of the "Saeculares" series of Philip (I) the Arab. Similarly, the elephant was popular as a coin type during the first half of the 3rd century, appearing on pieces of Caracalla, Geta, and Philip (I) the Arab. To celebrate the millennium (1000th anniversary) of the foundation of Rome in 247-248 Philip (I) the Arab staged magnificent games in which many wild beasts were exhibited in the arenas of the capital. This resulted in a series of coins featuring a gazelle, a European elk (moose), a hippopotamus, a stag, a goat (or hind), and an antelope, in addition to the lion already mentioned. The city's emblem, the she-wolf (suckling the twins Romulus and Remus), also appears as part of this series.

A few years later, during the sole reign of Gallienus (260-268), an extensive series of "animal" reverses was featured on the debased antoninianus denomination. Subsequent to this date such types appear far less frequently and are, in the main, restricted to the "legionary badge" issues of the usurpers Victorinus in Gaul and Carausius in Britannia. The charismatic Carausius also had types showing the milking of a cow, a griffin, and the traditional wolf and twins device.

In the late Empire, the wolf and twins appear on coins of another usurper, Maxentius (306-312), whose policy was to try to revive the past glories of the Imperial capital; and several decades later on small billon pieces (reduced centenionales) of the time of Constantine and his successors. In the mid 4th century a phoenix is shown on half maiorinae of Constantius II and Constans and a very fine representation of a bull, sometimes accompanied by an eagle, occurs on large billon pieces of Julian II towards the end of his short reign (360-67). One of the last animal representations on the Roman coinage is on a tiny bronze numnus of the easter Emperor Leo I (457-474) where a lion appears as a punning allusion to the emperor's name.

(To the right) Photograph of a large crowd of Jewish people marching through the Arch of Titus, taken by an anonym on the day of the Declaration of the Establishment of the State of Israel on 14 May 1948. Many of these people were European refugees who were living in camps for "displaced persons" waiting to resettle in Israel. Some people were holding banners in celebration. The message on the banner to the left of the photograph reads "Long Live the Hebrew State" (◊™◊ó◊ô ◊î◊û◊ì◊ô◊†◊î ◊î◊¢◊ë◊®◊ô◊™). It is no accident that the celebration was held by the Arch of Titus, a monument honouring the victory of Titus (Flavius Vespasianus; 39-81; r. 79-81) in Judaea in 70. The monument contains panels depicting the triumphal procession celebrated in 71 after the Roman victory in Judaea, culminating in the fall of Jerusalem. The south inner panel depicts the spoils taken from the (Second) Temple in Jerusalem, also known as the Herod's Temple, including the menorah. The menorah is often used as a symbol representing the Jewish people and Judaism in both the Land of Israel and the Diaspora. It eventually became the official emblem of the State of Israel after its establishment in 1948. The Arch of Titus became a symbol of the exile of the Jewish people, and until the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948 Jewish people followed a tradition not to pass under it. Once the State of Israel was established and the Jewish people could return to their homeland, the tradition was annulled. In fact, this photograph shows the Jewish people passing under the arch in the opposite direction to that of the exiles depicted on the arch. The marchers are seen walking in the direction of Jerusalem, as if they were choosing to leave Rome and declaring their victorious return to the Land of Israel after thousands of years of exile. The Arch of Titus has also provided the general model for many triumphal arches erected since the 16th century. It is for instance the inspiration for the Arc de Triomphe in Paris.

Titus (Flavius Vespasianus) was the elder son of Titus Flavius Vespasianus (9-79; r. 69-79), know as Vespasian, and Flavia Domitilla Major (12-69), known as Domitilla the Elder. He was a close boyhood friend of (Tiberius Claudius Caesar) Britannicus (41-55), son of the Emperor (Tiberius) Claudius (Caesar Augustus Germanicus; 10-54; r. 41-54), with whom he was educated. During the reign of Nero (Claudius Caesar Augustus Germanicus; 37-68; r. 54-68) he served as a military tribune in Germania and Britannia and in 67 accompanied his father to Judaea, where he commanded the Legio XV Apollinaris. When his father made his bid for the imperial throne in 69 Titus was given supreme command over the First Jewish‚ÄìRoman War. In September 70 he captured Jerusalem, thus effectively ending the Jewish Revolt which had begun four years earlier during the reign of Nero. Although he bore only the junior rank of Caesar throughout Vespasian's reign Titus was, in all other respects, his father's colleague in the rule of the Roman Empire. When Titus returned to Rome in June 71 they celebrated a magnificent joint triumph for the victory in Judaea and Titus was accorded a considerable share in the imperial coinage, with large scale issues in all metals. The share of his younger brother Titus Flavius Domitianus (51-96; r. 81-96), known as Domitian, was considerably smaller and there can be little doubt that the latter felt bitterly resentful of his inferior status (a resentment that was to find expression in the tyranny of Domitian's own reign a decade later). Titus suceeded to the imperial throne on the death of Vespasian on 23 June 79. He reigned for little more than two years, but this brief period witnessed a series of natural disasters. Most famous is the eruption of Vesuvius, which destroyed the towns of Pompeii and Herculaneum in 79, but in the following year in 80 the capital suffered from both plague and a devastating fire. It was in connection with these disasters that Titus issued a series of coins commemorating the services of prayer and propitiation through which he attempted to allay public alarm. One of the rare highlights of the reign was the dedication in June 80 of the great Amphitheatrum Flavium, known as the Colosseum. This magnificent arena, still one of the principal landmarks of modern Rome, had been commenced by Vespasian in 71 on the site of the stagnum (pool), formerly in the gardens of Nero's Golden House. The dedication by Titus was accompanied by lavish celebrations for the Roman populace. Probably exhausted and depressed by his ordeals, Titus died on 13 September 81 at the early age of forty-two. He was succeeded by his younger brother Domitian who, as heir to the throne, had been accorded a considerable coinage in all metals during the reign of Titus.
