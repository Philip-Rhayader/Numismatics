Note:

As coinage in the name of Caracalla did not commence until 196 his issues from the temporary eastern mints are limited to those of Laodicea in Syria and the enigmatic cistophori from the unidentified city in Asia. After the closure of Laodicea in 202 all of Caracalla's subsequent coinage is from the mint of Rome, eastern issues not resuming until the rebellion of Elagabalus against Caracalla's successor Macrinus. The portraiture displayed on Caracalla's coinage is of considerable interest, as it follows closely his physical development from a young boy of eight through adolescence and early manhood, to the full maturity of his final years. That of his younger brother shows a similar progression, though in his case it does not become so fully developed. Geta being only in his 23rd year at the time of his murder.

In 215, during his sole reign, Caracalla reformed the monetary system to finance ever increasing imperial expenditures by introducing the antoninianus, named after Caracalla's official name "Antoninus". The newly introduced antoninianus had the same silver purity as the denarius (51.5%) but weighed 5.1 g, compared to 3.23 g for the denarius. Although its silver content was only equivalent to about one and a half denarii, the antoninianus was apparently officially tariffed as the equivalent of two. For this reason, it is sometimes referred to as "double-denarius". This new coin always shows the emperor wearing the radiate crown instead of the laurel wreath of the denarius. In the case of empresses, the coin is distinguished by the addition of a crescent placed beneath the bust. It was initially mostly silver, but was slowly debased, following the trend of the debasement of silver during the Severan dynasty (193-235), to bronze with a minimal silver content (see Pense, 1992). Elagabalus demonetised the antoninianus in 219 in favour of the denarius. The antoninianus was re-introduced in 238 by Gordian III who, in turn, saw to it intentionally or otherwise that the denarius would be phased out for good.
Historical notes:

Marcus Aurelius Antoninus (born on 4 April 188 in Lugdunum; accession on 28 January 198; died on 8 April 217)

(To the top right) Bust of Caracalla, sculpted by an unknown artist between 212 and 215, displayed in the Palazzo Massimo of the Museo Nazionale Romano in Rome

Born Lucius Septimius Bassianus, the elder of the two sons of (Lucius) Septimius Severus (145-211; r. 193-211) and Julia Domna (circa 160-217; r. 193-211), was born on 4 April 188 at Lugdunum (now Lyon), where his father was serving as governor of Gallia Lugdunensis. The name Bassianus was that of his maternal grandfather, who was high priest of the sun-god Elah-Gabal at the Syrian city of Emesa. The nickname "Caracalla" or "Caracallus" derived from a hooded cloak of Gallic origin which he adopted as his favourite dress during his later years. Towards the end of 195 Septimius Severus (who had ascended the Imperial throne two and a half years before) adopted himself into the Antonine dynasty and changed the name of his elder son to Marcus Aurelius Antoninus, raising him to the rank of Caesar. This, of course, led to civil war with the existing Caesar, (Decimus) Clodius Albinus (circa 150-197), who had been given the junior Imperial title soon after Septimius Severus' accession with the promise of eventual inheritance of the throne. Following Septimius Severus' capture of the Parthian capital of Ctesiphon early in 198, Caracalla was further advanced to the rank of Augustus and co-emperor, his younger brother Geta (189-211; r. 209-211) taking his place as Caesar at the same time. Even though he was less than a year younger than Caracalla, Geta was not made Augustus for a further eleven years, until 209. This was a serious mistake on Septimius Severus' part, which doubtless exacerbated the growing rivalry and mutual antipathy of the two brothers. On the return of the Imperial family to Rome in 202, following an extended absence in the East, the 14-year-old Caracalla was married to (Publia Fulvia) Plautilla (?-211; r. 202-205), daughter of the influential praetorian prefect (Gaius or Lucius Fulvius) Plautianus (circa 150-205). The young man hated his father-in-law and disliked his haughty daughter, so it may be doubted whether the 3-year marriage was ever consummated. Plautilla shared her father's downfall in 205 though, unlike the prefect, escaped with her life for the time being.

In 208 the entire Imperial family departed for Britannia where they were to remain until Septimius Severus death early in 211. Caracalla gained much military experience at this time campaigning with his father in the wild country of Caledonia, far beyond the northern frontier of the Roman province. As Septimius Severus' health continued to decline his elder son played an increasingly prominent role in the prosecution of the war whilst Geta remained further south gaining administrative experience in Ebaracum (now York) and Londinium (now London), the provincial capital. All three emperors, however, received the title of Britannicus in honour of the victories over the barbarians of the north.

With the removal of their father's firm hand in February 211 the fraternal strife of Caracalla and Geta escalated rapidly. On their return to Rome they physically divided the palace between them, posting armed guards at every crossing point. This absurd situation clearly could not last and near the end of the year (probably on 26 December 211) Geta was lured to a meeting of reconciliation by his brother during the course of which he was treacherously murdered. After a mass extermination of thousands of those who had supposedly supported his younger brother, Caracalla spent much of his half decade of sole rule away from the capital. He campaigned in Germania in 213-214, receiving the title Germanicus for his achievements, and then headed east for his projected conquest of Parthia. Driven by a crazed obsession to emulate Alexander III of Macedon (356-323 BC), known as Alexander the Great, his physical and mental health began to deteriorate and he even visited the famous shrine of Asklepios at Pergamum in the hope of finding cures for his numerous ailments. In 215 he established his military headquarters at the Syrian capital of Antioch, accompanied by his mother Julia Domna, who was by now virtually running the Imperial government. Rejected in his outlandish request to receive the hand in marriage of the daughter of the Parthian monarch Artabanus IV (?-224; r. 213-224), Caracalla prepared to attack the rival empire. It is doubtful whether he would have achieved any spectacular and lasting conquests, but in any case the opportunity was denied him by the assassin's hand on the road between the Mesopotamian cities of Edessa and Carrhoe on 8 April 217. He had reigned for almost two decades and was still under 30 years of age. Perhaps his most notable act ‚Äì whatever the motives ‚Äì was the granting of Roman citizenship to virtually every free inhabitant of the Empire under the terms of the Constitutio Antoniniana.

Joseph Markowitz was born into a Jewish family in Hungary and later moved to the town of Vr≈°ac, then part of the Kingdom of Yugoslavia, in what is today Serbia. Together with his relatives, he was involved in a family firm known as Markowitz Brothers, active in the fur trade and leather tanning. It was in this setting ‚Äì shaped by enterprise, family tradition, and cultural exchange ‚Äì that his interest in ancient coins began to take root.

In 1930, he married Rosa, and the family later relocated to Belgrade amid growing political tensions. At some point before the outbreak of war, Joseph Markowitz began building a coin collection, focusing particularly on Roman silver. When he was drafted into the Yugoslav army, he hid the collection in the basement of the family home ‚Äì a quiet act of foresight in uncertain times.

During the war, Joseph Markowitz was taken prisoner by German forces. Rosa, left alone with their young son Chaim, was forced to flee Belgrade as the persecution of Jews intensified. Like so many Jewish families in the region, they were escaping the horrors of the Holocaust, which would ultimately lead to the annihilation of the Jewish community in Vr≈°ac ‚Äì a community that did not survive.

The family home in Belgrade was later destroyed during bombing raids. After the war, Rosa and Chaim returned to the devastated city. In the rubble of their former home, they searched for what remained ‚Äì and incredibly, in the ruins of the basement, they found the hidden coin collection, still intact. It had survived when so much else had been lost.

In 1949, the family emigrated to the newly founded State of Israel, bringing the rescued collection with them. They settled in Herzliya, where Joseph quietly rebuilt his life. He continued to care for and expand his collection with great dedication until his death in 1981.

The Joseph Markowitz Collection consists primarily of Roman silver coins, preserved over decades with scholarly care. It reflects not only Joseph Markowitz's love of history and ancient culture, but also a deeply personal journey ‚Äì shaped by displacement, resilience, and the will to remember.

Leu Numismatik AG offered the collection (702 individual lots and 16 larger lots) at auction on 5‚Äì8 July 2025, in the hopes that its dispersal might inspire other numismatists to continue in their efforts and to enable further study into history and ancient culture, and to share the story of a man who, in the face of devastation, carried forward a world that others tried to destroy ‚Äì and who chose to preserve knowledge, memory, and meaning through the language of coins.
