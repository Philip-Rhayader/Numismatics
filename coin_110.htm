Note:

The 2½-dollar quarter eagle was given its name in the Coinage Act of 1792, as a quarter of the ten-dollar eagle coin. It was struck from 1796 to 1929 at the main mint in Philadelphia (1796-1929), and branch mints in Charlotte (1838-1860), New Orleans (1839-1857), Dahlonega (1839-1859), San Francisco (1854-1879), and Denver (1911-1925). Years were skipped at the various mints, with no coins at all made from 1809 to 1820 and from 1916 to 1924. The first issues weighed 67.5 grains (4.37 g) at 916.7 fineness. This was modified to 64.5 grains (4.18 g) at 899.2 fineness by the Coinage Act of 1834. The Coinage Act of 1837 established a fineness of 900. Relatively few coins were struck prior to 1834, owing to their higher gold content (promoting melting for their bullion value).

The regular issue had four different designs over the years: the Capped Bust from 1796 to 1834 (with three varieties), the Classic Head from 1834 to 1839, the Liberty (or Coronet) Head from 1840 to 1907, and the Indian Head from 1908 to 1929. Liberty Head quarter eagles, minted nonstop from 1840 through 1907, are remarkable in American coinage as having the longest continuous production of any design without a major change. In essence, a Liberty Head quarter eagle of 1840 looks just like one of 1907, except for the date numerals.

The quarter eagle denomination was officially discontinued in 1933 with the Executive Order 6102 signed on 5 April 1933 by President Franklin (Delano) Roosevelt (1882-1945), forbidding "the hoarding of gold coin, gold bullion, and gold certificates within the continental United States", with some exceptions including "gold coins having recognized special value to collectors of rare and unusual coins". The Gold Reserve Act of 30 January 1934 required that all gold and gold certificates held by the Federal Reserve be surrendered and vested in the sole title of the Department of the Treasury. It also prohibited the Treasury and financial institutions from redeeming dollar bills for gold, established the Exchange Stabilization Fund under control of the Treasury to control the dollar's value without the assistance (or approval) of the Federal Reserve, and authorised the president to establish the gold value of the dollar by proclamation. Immediately following passage of the Act, the president changed the statutory price of gold from 20.67 to 35 dollar per troy ounce. This effectively devalued the dollar, reducing the amount of gold required to back the US currency and enabling the Federal Reserve to expand the money supply. The limitation on gold ownership in the United States was repealed after President Gerald (Rudolph) Ford (Jr.; 1913-2006) signed the International Development Association Appropriations Act of 1975, a rider to which legalised private ownership of gold coins, bars, and certificates, with effect on 31 December 1974.
Historical notes:

Libertas (Latin for "liberty") is the Roman goddess and personification of liberty.

(To the right) The Apotheosis of Washington, fresco painted by Constantino Brumidi (1805-1880) in 1865, and visible through the oculus of the dome in the rotunda of the United States Capitol Building in Washington, D.C. The first President of the United States, George Washington (1732-1799), is draped in purple, the colour worn by generals of the ancient Roman Republic during their triumphs, with a rainbow arch at his feet. He is flanked to his left by Victoria (Latin for "victory"), the Roman goddess and personification of victory, who is draped in green, blowing a horn, and to his right by Libertas (Latin for "liberty"), the Roman goddess and personification of liberty. Liberty wears a red liberty cap, symbolising emancipation, from a Roman tradition where slaves being manumitted would be given a felt cap. She holds a fasces in her right hand and an open book in the other, to which George Washington gestures with his right hand.
