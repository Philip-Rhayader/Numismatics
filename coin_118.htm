Note:

The coinage of the Mamlūk Sultanate (r. 1250-1517) followed that of their predecessors, consisting of gold dinars, silver dirhams and copper fulūs, and was produced in large volume during the course of their 250+ -year rule. The coins of the first Mamlūk sultans resembled those of the last sultans of the Ayyūbid Sultanate (r. 1171-1260) and were struck in Egypt at the two establishments of the Dar al-Darb (Arabic for "Mint") in Cairo (the most important mint of the realm) and Alexandria.

(Al-Malik al-Ẓāhir Rukn al-Dīn) Baybars (I) (r. 1260-1277) began the Mamlūk coinage in its proper form. Its most conspicuous feature is the blason, at the beginning present in gold, silver and copper alike under Baybars, on gold and silver under his son (al-Sa'īd Nāṣir al-Dīn) Baraka Qān (r. 1277-1279), and later on the copper only, with one or two exceptions.

The separate marginal legends have disappeared and been replaced by circular inscriptions which are now part of the field itself. To this type belong silver coins starting with Baybars and ending with (Al-Manṣūr Ḥusām al-Dīn) Lājīn (r. 1296-1299) and (al-Muẓaffar Rukn al-Dīn) Baybars II (r. 1309-1310). Under al-Nāṣir (Nāṣir al-Dīn) Muḥammad (r. 1293-1294; 1299-1309; 1310-1341) these smaller circular legends also disappear and the writing is now arranged in several horizontal lines which cover the entire field, on the gold as well as on the silver.

Simultaneously with the ordinary round-flan dirhams, so-called cut dirhams also circulated freely. The cut dirham, invented by the Fāṭimids (r. 909-1171) and adopted by the Ayyūbids, have also been taken over by the Mamlūks. But whereas the Ayyūbid cut dirham was a servile imitation of the irregularly-shaped Fāṭimid "black" dirham of base metal, the Mamlūks made theirs a little larger, heavier and of a better alloy. The dies with which they were struck, were those of the regular round-flan dirham, so that only a small portion of the coin legends was impressed on the flan. Entire hoards of these cut (square-flan) dirhams have been found; most of them belong to Baybars and al-Nāṣir Muḥammad, but those of other sultans have also been observed.

The copper fulūs occured in great numbers and in a broad spectrum of different varieties. The Egyptian issues are nearly always purely epigraphical, at first struck on a small flan with only short legends, but later on a wide flan with elaborate inscriptions. In Syria the flan is always of smaller size, the field ornamented and contains very frequently some heraldic charge. These heraldic coins contribute greatly to the knowledge of the Mamlūk blason. Amongst the Egyptian emissions only one series, belonging to al-Nāṣir Muḥammad, presents a blason.

From the rule of Baybars reign through the second rule of (Al-Ẓāhir Sayf al-Dīn) Barqūq (r. 1382-1389; 1390-99), most gold coins were of high purity and struck to random weights and traded by weight. These are technically known as "trade dinars". Their weights vary from less than 3 grammes to 15 grammes, with exceptional examples surpassing 18 grammes. From the reign of (al-Ashraf Sayf al-Dīn) Barsbāy (r. 1422-1438), most issues were struck to a standard comparable with the Venetian ducat, and are known as "ashrafīs", from the laqab of Barsbāy, al-Malik al-Ashraf. The ashrafī weighed about 3.4 grammes and hence was somewhat lighter than the ducat (about 3.5 grammes). The weight and purity of the ashrafī were generally carefully maintained, though the purity began to drop in the final decades. Between the end of the second rule of Barqūq and the rule of Barsbāy is a period of transition, during which several short-lived gold issues of varying size, design and weight were minted.

Silver coins were struck from alloyed metal (most often about ⅔ fine) until the introduction of a pure silver coinage during the interlude of al-Musta'īn (bi'llāh Abū al-Faḍl 'Abbās) (r. 1412), whose dirhams were struck to a standard of about 2.8 grammes (thus somewhat lighter than the canonical dirham of 2.97 grammes). The weight standard was subsequently gradually lowered, falling to slightly over one gramme by the end of the sultanate. However, silver coins retain a high degree of purity, though some later issues of al-Ashraf Qānṣūh al-Ghūri (r. 1501-1516) exhibit some modest debasement of the silver content. In nearly all periods, silver seems to have circulated far more widely in Syria than in Egypt.

Copper coinage was an important component of Mamlūk coinage until the end of the first rule of (al-Nāṣir Abū al-Sa'ādāt) Faraj (r. 1399-1405), particularly during the 14th century. From the rule of Baybars through the third reign of al-Nāṣir Muḥammad, the surviving record is spotty, to say the least. In Alexandria, copper was apparently only struck in the last three decades of the fourteenth century. Copper was little used after the rule of Faraj until the the rule of Al-Ashraf (Sayf al-Dīn) Qā'itbāy (1468-1496), whereafter it again became common, though almost exclusively in Egypt. Balog (1964) has examined the remarkable use of heraldry on Mamlūk coppers, though quite a few more heraldic types have subsequently been discovered.

The Mamlūks operated just eight mints during their 250+ -year rule. The six principal mints are al-Qāhirah (Cairo) and al-Iskandarīyah (Alexandria; closed after the death of al-Mu'ayyad (Abū al-Naṣr) Shaykh in 1421) in Egypt, together with Dimishq (Damascus), Ḥalab (Aleppo), Ḥamāh and Ṭarāblus (Tripoli) in Syria. These six mints struck coins in all three metals, though not for every period (gold coins of Ḥamāh and Ḥamāh, as well as silver coins of al-Iskandarīyah, are very rare). The other two mints were temporary mints: al-Lāḏiqiyya (Lattakia) in Syria (circa 1330-circa 1380) and Malat'ia (Malatya) in Anatolia (circa 1380-circa 1400). Coins of these two temporary mints are rare. In addition, some rare coins were struck by various Anatolian rulers in the name of one or another Mamlūk ruler, often with mint name and/or date. Though properly regarded as coins of the various Anatolian rulers, they are generally categorised under the Mamlūks for the sake of convention and convenience.
Historical notes:

al-Malik al-Ẓāhir Rukn al-Dīn Baybars (I) (الملك الظاهر ركن الدين ببرس) (born in 625 AH (12 December 1227–29 November 1228); proclaimed sultan soon after 25 Ramadān AH 658 (3 September 1260); died on 27 Muḥarram AH 676 (30 June 1277))

(To the right) Photograph of the eastern side of the Baybars Bridge (modern Jisr Jindas; Arabic for "Jindas Bridge") in Lod, Israel, taken by an anonym in 2010. Baybars brought Lod under Mamlūk control by AH 672 (1267–1268) and erected this bridge in the same year as part of the establishment of a new postal service ("barīd" in Arabic) between Cairo and Damascus. The bridge is believed to be constructed on Roman foundations, using masonry from the Crusader Cathedral that was built on the site of the Church of Saint George and had been destroyed in 1191 after the Crusaders were defeated by the Ayyūbids at the decisive Battle of Hattin in 1187. On the western and eastern sides of the bridge are two nearly identical inscriptions, flanked by two lions (the blason of Baybars). The inscription on the eastern side reads as follows: "In the name of the kind and merciful God, whose blessings be on our Lord Muhammad, his family, and his companions. The building of this blessed bridge was ordered by our master, the great Sultan al-Malik al-Ẓāhir Rukn al-Dīn Baybars, son of 'Abd Allāh, in the time of his son our Lord Sultan al-Malik al-Sa'īd Nāṣir al-Dīn Baraka Qān, may God glorify their victories and grant them His grace. And this, under the direction of the humble servant aspiring to the mercy of God, Ala al-Dīn Ali al-Suwwaq, may God grant grace to him and his parents; in the month Ramadān AH 671." Ala al-Dīn Ali al-Suwwaq (?-?) was also charged three years earlier with overseeing the construction of a congregational mosque on the site of the demolished Church of Saint George/Crusader Cathedral.

The Mamlūk Sultanate (r. 1250-1517) ruled Egypt, and the historical regions of Syria and Hejaz for over 250 years, with Cairo as its capital. It was ruled by a military caste of mamlūks (the Arabic word for "slaves") headed by a sultan. Mamlūks were purchased as slaves while still young males, and trained for various duties in service to the sultanate. When their training was completed, they were manumitted and given a role in the Sultanate administration or army. Some would eventually be elected to the office of sultan. Most of the Mamlūks were imported from Russia and Central Asia. In practice, however, most of the Mamlūk sultans, especially during the early period of the Sultanate, were the sons or brothers of previous sultans.The sultanate was established with the overthrow of the Ayyūbid dynasty (r. 1171-1260) in Egypt in 1250 and was conquered by the Ottoman Empire (circa 1299-1922) in 1517.

The Mamlūk Sultanate is traditionally divided into two dynasties, the Turkic or Bahri dynasty (r. 1250-1390) and the Circassian or Burji dynasty (1382-1517), with a short overlap due to the second rule of Al-Ṣāliḥ (Ṣalāḥ al-Dīn) Ḥājji II (r. 1281–1282; 1289-1290). The term "Bahri" means "of the sea" and refers to the insular fortress on the island of al-Rawda within the Nile River in Cairo, where the Bahri dynasty originally settled, whereas the terms "Burji" means "of the tower" and refers to the inland Citadel of Cairo, used by the Burji dynasty as their principal fortress. Whereas the Bahri rulers allowed hereditary succession, the Burji rulers were chosen by the military leadership, who selected candidates from amongst its own ranks and eschewed hereditary succession.

Al-Malik al-Ẓāhir Rukn al-Dīn Baybars (I) (r. 1260-1277), commonly known as Baybars and nicknamed Abu al-Futuh (Arabic for "Father of Conquests"), was the fourth Mamlūk sultan, of Kipchak (Turkic nomadic tribe) origin, in the Bahri dynasty, succeeding al-Muẓaffar (Sayf al-Dīn) Quṭuz (r. 1259-1260). He was one of the commanders of the Muslim forces that inflicted a defeat on the Seventh Crusade of the French king Louis IX (known as saint Louis; r. 1226-1270). He also led the vanguard of the Mamlūk army at the Battle of Ain Jalut in 1260, which marked the first substantial defeat of the Mongol army, which is considered a turning point in history.

The reign of Baybars marked the start of an age of Mamlūk dominance in the Eastern Mediterranean and solidified the durability of their military system. He managed to pave the way for the end of the Crusader presence in the Levant and reinforced the union of Egypt and Syria as the region's pre-eminent Muslim state, able to fend off threats from both Crusaders and Mongols, and even managed to subdue the Nubian kingdom of Makuria, which was famous for being unconquerable by previous Muslim empire invasion attempts. As sultan, Baybars also engaged in a combination of diplomacy and military action, allowing the Mamlūks of Egypt to greatly expand their empire.
