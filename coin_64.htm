This is the first coinage to bear (Julius) Caesar's name. These coins were produced in huge numbers (several millions) in order to finance Caesar's military operations after he crossed the Rubicon and invaded Italy from the north in January 49 BC, driving Pompey (the Great) from Rome to seek refuge in Greece. While the size of this production could raise eyebrows, it must be remembered that by the end of his tenure in Gaul Caesar had no fewer than eight legions of about 5,000 men expecting payment, and that the gross salary for an ordinary legionary was 225 denarii per year. Still, minting several millions of coins required vast amount of bullion. As pointed out by Nousek (2008), and as evidence of a proposed minting date in early 49 BC, one need not look far to find a probable source. On 1st April 49 BC, Caesar addressed the remnants of the Senate and the Concilium Plebis (People's Assembly), promising grain distribution and a gift of 75 denarii per man. He then proceeded to the treasury at the Temple of Saturn and demanded access to the aerarium sanctius, the special "emergency" reserve. Although he met with stubborn opposition, Caesar managed to make off with 15,000 bars of gold, 30,000 bars of silver, and a great quantity of silver coins. This purloined bullion is likely to be the source of silver for this coin issue.

Despite being the most widespread of all Caesar‚Äôs coins, the design of this type is still somewhat mysterious and subject to interpretation (see Rowan, 2019, for a summary of possible interpretations). Authorities have even debated which side of the coin is which: RCC1 describes the side with the elephant as the obverse, but other experts dispute this. The symbolism of this side of the coin is commonly held to show the triumph of good, represented as an elephant, over evil, represented as a "horned serpent". Alternatively, the "horned serpent" may be intended to represent a carnyx, a S-shaped trumpet with a bell styled in the shape of the head of some animal, here a horned serpent, used by the Celtic tribes in Gaul, whom Caesar had recently overcome in his epic eight-year conquest. In this case the elephant would again represent Caesar himself, or the unstoppable juggernaut of Rome. Unlike Pompey, Caesar brazenly placed his own name on the coinage without having the constitutional authority to do so, as Lucius Cornelius Sulla Felix (138-78 BC), commonly known as Sulla, had done 33 years before, in 82 BC. The other side of the coin depicts pontifical implements, which symbolise the prestigious lifetime office of pontifex maximus that he had held since 63 BC.

Varieties:

There are two main varieties, for which the elephant is realistic or cartoonish. One speculation is that the cartoonish elephant was minted in Spain.

RCC1 noted 750 obverse dies and 833 reverse dies. The question of the number of coins that could be struck from a single ancient die before it was discarded is contentious. Modern experiments suggest that an obverse die could strike about 15,000 acceptable coins, a reverse die only about half as many. However, often more than two reverse dies were used with an obverse die, since dies were often replaced before they wore out. Using a conservative figure of 10,000 coins per die, the production of this denarius type would be 7.5 million denarii for 750 obverse dies.
Note:

The Roman Imperatorial period commences with the outbreak of civil war between (Julius) Caesar (100-44 BC) and Pompey (the Great; 106-48 BC) at the beginning of 49 BC and ends about twenty-two years later on 16 January 27 BC when Caesar's heir Gaius Octavius (-63-14), also known as Octavian, was given the name Augustus and effectively became the autocratic ruler of the Roman Empire. The Imperatorial coinage represents the transitional stage between the traditional Republican series and the revolutionary new Imperial coinage of Augustus and his successors.

In its earlier phase (49-45 BC) the Imperatorial series comprised issues bearing the names, though not yet the portraits, of the principal contenders for power. These issues existed side by side with the regular coinage of the Republican moneyers at the Capitoline mint in Rome, though in reality these were nothing more than a propaganda tool in the hands of Caesar and his successors who controlled the output of the Capitoline mint almost from the start of the civil war down to the closure of the establishment by Octavian in 40 BC. Additional coinages were produced at various provincial locations by supporters of Pompey (the Great) and his sons. A major turning point came at the beginning of 44 BC, when Caesar took the unprecedented step of placing his own portrait on the coinage. His assassination followed almost exactly three months later but, once initiated, this revolutionary innovation was irrevocable and portraiture rapidly became the norm, thus paving the way for the coinage of the Roman Empire.

In the post-Caesarian phase of the Imperatorial coinage (44-27 BC) issues initially were divided between the faction of the Second Triumvirate, led by Marcus Antonius (83-30 BC), known in English as Mark Antony, Octavian and (Marcus Aemilius) Lepidus (circa 89-13 BC), and the Republicans, most prominent amongst whom were the tyrannicides (Marcus Junius) Brutus (circa 85-42 BC) and his brother-in-law (Gaius) Cassius (Longinus; circa 86-42 BC). After the pivotal battle of Philippi in the autumn of 42 BC only the two principal Triumvirs, Mark Antony and Octavian, remained to contend for supreme authority in the Roman state. That struggle was finally resolved by the battle of Actium in 31 BC and the subsequent conquest the following year of Mark Antony's last strong-hold, the Ptolemaic kingdom of Egypt.

The Imperatorial coinage subdivides conveniently into four major categories: (1) issues of Pompey (the Great), his sons and their adherents; (2) issues of (Julius) Caesar; (3) issues of the Republican tyrannicides and their adherents; and (4) issues of the Triumvirs and their adherents. Between the years 49 and 40 BC it is important to remember that these essentially military coinages must be viewed in association with the contemporary coinage of the Roman moneyers. These were augmented, between 43 and 41 BC, by an exceptional output of moneyers' gold.
Historical notes:

(Gaius) Julius Caesar (born on 13 July 100 BC in Rome; dictator from 49 BC; died on 15 March 44 BC)

(To the right) Caesar on his chariot, painted by Andrea Mantegna (circa 1431-1506) between the mid-1480s and 1506. This painting is the final painting of a series of nine known as "The Triumphs of Caesar", which can be counted amongst the finest achievements in Italian Renaissance art. They were probably painted for Francesco II Gonzaga (1466-1519), who became the 4th Marquis of Mantua in Italy in 1484. The nine paintings form a sequence, which should really be called the "Triumph of Caesar", because it depicts a single procession, culminating in this final episode ‚Äì the arrival of Caesar borne on a chariot passing in front of a triumphal arch. The procession cannot be connected with a particular campaign, which is probably why it was seen more generally to celebrate the triumphs of Caesar as an ancient Roman soldier, statesman and historian revered during the Renaissance. "The Triumphs of Caesar" were acquired by the british monarch Charles I (1600-1649; r. 1625-1649) in 1629 when he purchased a large part of the collections formed by the Gonzaga family. They were clearly then considered to be the masterpiece of the collection. The paintings were hung at Hampton Court Palace near London soon after the probable date of their arrival in England in 1630. Since then, they have seldom left Hampton Court Palace, but their dedicated gallery is now undergoing refurbishment (with its completion planned for 2026). Having been generously loaned by the british monarch Charles III (1948-Present; r. 2022-Present), the first six of the nine paintings are now on display in The National Gallery in London, in newly made frames that echo the original arrangement in Mantua. The seventh canvas is off-display, to be analysed in The National Gallery‚Äôs conservation studio, while the final two are on view in the Queen‚Äôs Presence Chamber in Hampton Court Palace.

(Julius) Caesar pursued the traditional cursus honorum of the young aristocrat and did not rise to political prominence until the latter part of the 60s BC. He was elected to the prestigious lifetime office of pontifex maximus in 63 BC and the next year held a praetorship followed by a successful military command in Spain. Caesar was now poised to occupy the highest magistracy of the Roman Republic and in 59 BC he embarked on the first of his five consulships.

Being now in a position to take the initiative in the political game being played out in Rome he proposed a coalition between himself, Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus (106-48 BC), known in English as Pompey (the Great), and (Marcus Licinius) Crassus (115-53 BC), which has come to be known as the First Triumvirate. Although not an officially constituted body, like the Second Triumvirate sixteen years later, this alliance between the three most powerful and influential men in Rome was a force to be reckoned with and one that could not easily be opposed, even by the Senate. It led to Caesar obtaining a 5-year proconsular command in Gaul, which was to provide him with the military prestige and resources that he needed to make his ultimate bid for supreme power a decade later.

The delicate balance of power was fatally disrupted in 53 BC when Crassus was defeated and killed in the East during his invasion of Parthian Mesopotamia. Caesar and Pompey were now left to face one another as rivals for supremacy in the Roman world. A build-up of tensions starting in late 50 BC, with both Caesar and Pompey refusing to back down, led to the outbreak of Caesar's civil war. Pompey and his allies induced the Senate to demand Caesar give up his provinces and armies in early January 49 BC. Matters escalated a few days later when Caesar refused and instead crossed the Rubicon and invaded Italy from the north, driving Pompey from Rome to seek refuge in Greece. The eventual outcome was Caesar's victory over his rival at Pharsalus on 9 August 48 BC, resulting in Pompey's political downfall and assassination a few weeks later. The struggle between Caesar and the Pompeians, led by the late triumvir's generals and his two sons, continued for several years and was only finally resolved on the battlefield of Munda in Spain in March 45 BC. Caesar's political pre-eminence was now undisputed and at the beginning of the year 44 BC the dictator gave orders for his portrait to be placed on the regular silver coinage of Rome.

Undoubtedly, this development was instrumental in fueling the grievances of those republicans who feared that Caesar was aiming at the establishment of a monarchy. Accordingly, on the Ides (15th) of March 44 BC he was struck down by a conspiracy of senators led by (Marcus Junius) Brutus (circa 85-42 BC) and his brother-in-law (Gaius) Cassius (Longinus; circa 86-42 BC). He had adopted as his heir Gaius Octavius (-63-14), the grandson of his sister Julia (Minor; before 100-51 BC). Remarkably, the young man was willing to take up the heavy mantle of his great-uncle's political ambitions and was destined to become the founder of autocratic government in Rome under the name Augustus.
