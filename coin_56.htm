Note:

Following the trend of the debasement of silver introduced by Nero in 64, Marcus Aurelius further devalued the denarius upon his accession in 161 to pay for constant wars and increased expenses. The silver content of the denarius was decreased from 83.5% to 79%, the silver weight dropping from 2.68 g to 2.57 g. In 168, he revalued the denarius, increasing its silver content from 79% to 82%, the silver weight increasing from 2.57 g to 2.67 g. However, two years later he reverted to the previous values because of the military crises facing the empire.

Because of the unprecedented length of time during which Marcus Aurelius bore the junior Imperial rank of Caesar (22 years) his coinage under Antoninus is abundant in the full range of denominations. During his own reign Marcus struck additional issues in the names of his wife Faustina Junior, his daughter Lacilla, his co-emperors Lucius Verus and, later, Commodus, and posthumous issues for Divus Antoninus (161), Divus Verus (169) and Diva Faustina (176). At the very end of the reign (from 178) coins were struck for Crispina, wife of the young co-emperor Commodus. However, as her coinage continued into the sole reign of her husband all issues are normally associated with Commodus.
Historical notes:

Marcus Aurelius (Antoninus) (born on 26 April 121 in Rome; accession on 7 March 161; died on 17 March 180)

(To the right) Busts of the Co-emperors Marcus Aurelius and Lucius Verus, sculpted by an unknown artist between 161 and 170, displayed in Room 70 of the British Museum in London

Originally named Marcus Annius Catilius Severus, the future Emperor Marcus Aurelius Antoninus was born in Rome in 121 and was the nephew of the Empress Faustina Senior, wife of Antoninus Pius (138-161). Following the early death of his father Marcus Annius Verus, brother of the Empress Faustina Senior, in 124, he was brought up in the household of their father and his grandfather, also named Marcus Annius Verus, who had an illustrious public career and was three times consul, a rare distinction for someone outside the Imperial family. The Emperor Hadrian (117-138), who adopted Antoninus Pius, recognised the fine qualities of the youth and arranged for his betrothal to the daughter of Aelius Caesar, who he had also adopted. Later following the death of Aelius Caesar in 138, Hadrian stipulated that the new Caesar, Antoninus Pius, should adopt Marcus Aurelius together with Aelius Caesar's son, Lucius Verus. On the accession of Antoninus Pius in the summer of 138 Marcus Aurelius' career was rapidly advanced and he was even promoted to the rank of Caesar in 139, a clear indication that the new emperor regarded him, rather than Lucius Verus, as the heir to the Imperial throne. The annulment of Marcus Aurelius' betrothal to the daughter of Aelius Caesar provided additional confirmation of Antoninus Pius' intentions, as the young man was now betrothed to the emperor's daughter, Faustina the Younger. In 145 the couple were married and two years later the 26-year-old Caesar was granted the tribunician power. Antoninus Pius survived to the age of 74 and Marcus Aurelius was already in his fortieth year by the time he ascended the throne at his death in 161.

Loyal to the memory of the Emperor Hadrian, one of his first acts as emperor was to associate the younger Lucius Verus with him as co-ruler, thus inaugurating the first joint reign in almost two hundred years of Imperial history. Unfortunately, the experiment was not a resounding success. Lucius Verus proved to be weak and addicted to the pursuit of pleasure and was thus more of a liability than an asset in the demanding task of governing a vast Empire. This merely confirmed Antoninus Pius' earlier judgement and the sudden and untimely death of Lucius Verus in 169 must have come as a considerable relief to Marcus Aurelius. Although gentle and philosophical by nature, Marcus Aurelius was fated to spend long periods of his reign enduring the harsh conditions of military camp life. Throughout the 170s he undertook arduous campaigns on the Rhine and Danube frontiers, where there was much unrest amongst the barbarian tribes, and it was during this trying time that he wrote his celebrated "Meditations" based on the principles of Stoic philosophy. Following the abortive revolt in the East of the general Avidius Cassius in 175, Marcus Aurelius decided to advance his young son Commodus to Imperial status, first as Caesar (175) and then as Augustus and co-emperor late in the following year. Worn out by the cares of state Marcus Aurelius died at the age of 59 in 180 and was succeeded by the 19-year-old co-emperor Commodus. He was immediately deified, an extensive posthumous coinage being issued in his honour. A careful, generous and conscientious autocrat, it has been written of Marcus Aurelius that "in the evening of Rome's greatness her ruler fittingly personified the virtues that had been her glory".
