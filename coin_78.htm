Note:

In 215 Caracalla reformed the monetary system to finance ever increasing imperial expenditures by introducing the antoninianus, named after Caracalla's official name "Antoninus". The newly introduced antoninianus had the same silver purity as the denarius (51.5%) but weighed 5.1 g, compared to 3.23 g for the denarius. Although its silver content was only equivalent to about one and a half denarii, the antoninianus was apparently officially tariffed as the equivalent of two. For this reason, it is sometimes referred to as "double-denarius". This new coin always shows the emperor wearing the radiate crown instead of the laurel wreath of the denarius. In the case of empresses, the coin is distinguished by the addition of a crescent placed beneath the bust. It was initially mostly silver, but was slowly debased, following the trend of the debasement of silver during the Severan dynasty (193-235), to bronze with a minimal silver content (see Pense, 1992). Elagabalus demonetised the antoninianus in 219 in favour of the denarius. The antoninianus was re-introduced in 238 by Gordian III who, in turn, saw to it intentionally or otherwise that the denarius would be phased out for good.

Rome remained the principal mint for the production of coinage throughout this reign, though in the minting of antoniniani it was assisted at various times by at least two, and possibly three, provincial mints. One of these was at Viminacium, an important city of the Danubian province of Upper Moesia, though the rare products of this mint have only recently been identified. Another mint was located at Antioch in Syria, though as under Gordian III the issues usually attributed to it fall into two distinct groups, one early (244-245), the other late (247-249). Stylistically these groups are quite different from one another, which has led some numismatists to believe that only the later group should be assigned to Antioch, whilst the earlier coinage should instead be given to the same uncertain eastern mint that had produced some of Gordian III's later coinage.

During the years 247 and 248 the "Saeculares" coinage struck to commemorate the millennium (1000th anniversary) of the foundation of Rome included the following reverse inscriptions: "SAECLARES AVGG" (the age of the Two Emperors), "SAECVLVM NOVVM" (the new age) and "MILIARIVM SAECVLVM" (the millenia). The "Saeculares" coinage is divided into two reverse series: the reverse animal series showing wild animals (with the inscription "SAECLARES AVGG" for all the denominations) and the reverse architectural series illustrating either a hexastyle temple (containing a seated statue of Roma) (with the inscription "SAECVLVM NOVVM" for the denominations in gold and silver and the inscription "SAECLARES AVGG" for the denominations in bronze) or a cippus (column) (with the inscription "SAECLARES AVGG" for all the denominations or the inscription "MILIARIVM SAECVLVM" for the denominations in bronze). The reverse animal series shows at least eight different wild animals: a lion, a she-wolf (suckling the twins Romulus and Remus), a gazelle, a European elk (moose), a hippopotamus, a stag, a goat (or hind), and an antelope. Struck in a wide range of denominations the series included the gold aureus, the silver antoninianius and quinarius, and the bronze sestertius, dupondius and as. Included also are issues of Otacilia Severa, wife of Philip (I) the Arab, and Philip (Il) the Younger, their son who had been raised to the rank of Augustus in 247.

The "Ludi Saeculares" (Secular Games) organised for the millennium of the foundation of Rome was highlighted by a splendid array of wild animals. These wild animals had originally been collected for the intended triumph of Gordian III in the Persian war, which saw his death and the associated proclamation of Philip (I) the Arab as emperor in 244. The animals were used by Philip (I) the Arab in magnificent displays and (slaughter) games put on in the key venues, like the Circus Maximus and the Colosseum. This large collection of exotic wild beasts included several specimens of antelope, elephant, European elk (moose), gazelle, giraffe, goat, hippopotamus, wild horse, hyena, leopard, lion, rhinoceros, stag, tiger and wild ass. It was these animals, collected for Gordian III, which, no doubt, inspired the many animal reverse types found on the "Saeculares" coinage struck to commemorate the millennium of the foundation of Rome. Besides, the she-wolf (suckling the twins Romulus and Remus) would have been recognised as the symbol of Rome rather than as a reminder of the games' arena.

A remarkable feature of this "Saeculares" coinage was the earliest appearance of officina (workshop) marks at the mint of Rome. The mint of Rome at this time was apparently divided into six officinae. These six officinae produced the "Saeculares" issue with marks as Roman numerals I to VI (only for the silver antoninianius) and are of much interest in that the eight animals illustrated on the reverse were divided among them with Officina I striking only the lion, II the lion and the she-wolf, III the gazelle and the European elk (moose), IV the hippopotamus, V the stag, and VI the goat (or hind) and the antelope. At almost the same time the same six officinae produced a different issue with marks as ancient Greek numerals Œë to ↅ (only for the silver antoninianius) rather than Roman numerals I to VI. Also, generally speaking, the six officinae were assigned to the production of issues in the name of a single imperial personage shown on the obverse: Officinae I/Œë, II/Œí, V/Œï and VI/ↅ minted for Philip (I) the Arab, Officina III/Œì minted for Philip (II) the Younger, and Officina IV/Œî minted for Otacilia Severa.

Although the practice of adding officina marks was not immediately adopted for succeeding issues, this development nevertheless marked an important numismatic turning point. Before long mint marks of increasing complexity began appearing not only on the issues of the mint of Rome itself, but also on those of the ever-expanding network of provincial mints.
Historical notes:

(Marcia) Otacilia Severa (born circa 220; given the title of Augusta in 244; died in or after 249)

(To the right) Bust of Otacilia Severa, sculpted by an unknown artist between 244 and 249, displayed in the Hall of the Machines of the Centrale Montemartini in Rome

Marcia Otacilia Severa married Philip (I) the Arab (circa 204-249; r. 244-249) in 234, though very little is known of the biographical details of her life. She may have been of noble Roman birth, as suggested by her name, though if so we can only speculate on how she came to mary the son of an Arab chieftain. She gave birth to their only son, Philip (II) the Younger, about 237 (that is about seven years before her husband's accession as Emperor) and was accorded the title of Augusta early in the reign in 244. She enjoyed a generous share of the imperial coinage, with one of the six officinae (workshops) of the Rome mint being assigned to the production of issues in her name. The Antioch mint also struck antoniniani for her, though these are by no means common. Otacilia's ultimate fate is shrouded in mystery. She was in Rome when her husband was defeated and killed at the Battle of Verona in 249 following a rebellion led by his successor, (Gaius Messius Quintus Trajanus) Decius (circa 201-251), also known as Trajan Decius. However, it is unclear whether she was murdered as their son Philip (II) the Younger by the Praetorians shortly after the death of her husband or merely permitted to live a private life in obscurity.
