Note:

Following the passing of Queen Elizabeth II (1926-2022; r. 1952-2022) on 8 September 2022, Prince Charles, Prince of Wales, acceded to the throne and became King Charles III. This called for a new set of definitive coins in the name of the new king. Following the tradition dating from the reign of King Charles II (1630-1685; r. 1660-1685) marking the Stuart Restoration in 1660, the coinage portrait of the new monarch will face in the opposite direction to that of the preceding monarch. Although the reason for this is not certain, it may have started because King Charles II wanted to turn his back on the Commonwealth of England, Scotland and Ireland, and its ruler and Lord Protector Oliver Cromwell (1599-1658) from 1653 to 1658, succeeded by his son Richard Cromwell (1626-1712) from 1658 to 1659. As Her Late Majesty Queen Elizabeth II faced right on her coinage, King Charles III is to face left on his coinage.

The first set of definitive coins was issued in 2023 and contains the eight denominations of the circulating coins of the United Kingdom: ¬£2, ¬£1, 50p, 20p, 10p, 5p, 2p and 1p. The 2023 definitive coins feature eight new designs, created by British sculptor Martin Jennings (1957-Present), which were unveiled on 12 October 2023. The reverse of the coins are inspired by British wildlife and highlight His Majesty King Charles III‚Äôs passion for wildlife conservation: flora that symbolise the four nations of the country (a rose for England, a daffodil for Wales, a thistle for Scotland and a shamrock for Northern Ireland) for the ¬£2 coin; two bees that symbolise the 250+ species of bee living in the country, including the bumblebee üêù, honey bee and mining bee, with the population of several species either endangered or vulnerable to extinction in the country, for the ¬£1 coin; the Atlantic salmon, which population is endangered in the wild in the country, for the 50p coin; a puffin, which is one of Britain‚Äôs most popular seabirds and which population is vulnerable to extinction in the country, for the 20p coin; a capercaillie, which is found in Scotland and is the world‚Äôs largest grouse, and which population is vulnerable to extinction in the country, for the 10p coin; a branch of oak tree, which supports more life than any other native tree species in the country, for the 5p coin; a red squirrel, which population is endangered in the country, for the 2p coin; a hazel dormouse, which population is endangered in the country, for the 1p coin. The designs were created with the support of the Royal Horticultural Society (RHS) and the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds (RSPB). The number indicating the value of each coin has also been enlarged, to help children understand the value of money. Unifying the new coins is a unique repeating pattern with a motif featuring three interlocking letters C, with a diamond (‚óÜ) at the centre of each quartet of adjacent motifs. This aspect of the design takes its inspiration from history and the cypher of Charles II (the letter C for Charles, two interlocking letters C for King Charles II, and three interlocking letters C for King Charles III), while the wildlife looks to the future and the importance of the natural world.

The 2023 definitive coin set was produced to the standards Brilliant Uncirculated in base metals and Proof in silver and gold. The coins of this set feature a special privy mark that takes the form of a Tudor Crown. This coin is a (die) "Trial piece" for the ¬£1 coin produced in 2024 to the standard Proof in silver, available only in the silver proof year set (with an edition limited to 500) featuring the 13 coins of the year 2024: ¬£5 Buckingham Palace, ¬£2 Sir Winston Churchill, ¬£2 The National Gallery, 50p Team GB and ParalympicsGB, ¬£50p Royal National Lifeboat Institution (RNLI), and the 8 definitive coins. The 2023 definitive ¬£1 coin entered circulation on 20 August 2024, in line with demand from UK banks and Post Offices, with an initial issue of 2.975 million coins out of a mintage of 10.03 million coins.

The "Trial pieces", such as this coin, are produced to the same standards as the coins issued by The Royal Mint. However because these "Trial pieces" are used in the coin production process they may show signs of this use. The "Trial pieces" play an essential role in developing potential new products and processes and are vital as part of the production process at the Royal Mint. Their use allows experimentation with different techniques and evaluation of the viability of a potential new product or process. There are usually between 7 and 30 "Trial pieces" produced for a product, depending on the complexity of the design. However, once the product is approved, most of the "Trial pieces" are destroyed and only a handful of pieces (usually around three) are catalogued for reference purposes. A single "Trial piece" from every product and test is kept for reference and safekeeping at The Royal Mint Museum. The engineering team often use "Trial pieces" from the past, some from decades or even centuries ago, as inspiration for new products. "Trial pieces" are also kept on-hand during the production run of new products as a reference point for the production team, so that they can maintain the quality expected of the finished product. Hence, these "Trial pieces" are normally extremely rare in relation to the number of corresponding production pieces. This coin is unique in that only one specimen was available for sale.

The regular dodecagonal (12-sided) ¬£1 coin entered circulation on 28 March 2017. It was introduced to replace the round ¬£1 coin first issued on 21 April 1983 because of its vulnerability to sophisticated counterfeiters. Approximately one in thirty round ¬£1 coins in circulation was a counterfeit before the introduction of the new regular dodecagonal ¬£1 coin. When it was announced, the Royal Mint claimed that it was "the most secure coin in the world". It has a number of features that make it much more difficult to counterfeit: a distinctive regular dodecagonal shape (making it instantly recognisable, even by touch), a bimetallic arrangement (with a silver-colored inner circle and a gold-colored outer ring), a shield-shaped lenticular feature with vertical lens direction (changing from the symbol ¬£ to the number 1 when the coin is tilted from side to side), micro-lettering on a narrow raised strip touching the listel on both sides of the coin ("ONE POUND ¬∑ ONE POUND" on the obverse and the year, for example "‚Äî2024‚Äî", on the reverse), segmented reeding on the edge (with grooves on alternate sides of the regular dodecagonal edge), and, according to The Royal Mint, a hidden high security feature built into the coin to protect it from counterfeiting in the future.
